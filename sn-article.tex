\title[FutureScope]{Diagnostic-First Time Series Forecasting with
Validated Residual Analysis and Automated Refitting}

\author{\fnm{Swastik} \sur{Vaish}}
\affil{\orgdiv{Computer and Communication Engineering}}

%%------------------------------------------------------------
\abstract{
Forecasting frameworks routinely report point-accuracy metrics while
omitting any validation of residual assumptions, leaving practitioners
unable to determine whether fitted models are correctly specified.
We present \textbf{FutureScope v2}, an open-source pipeline that
treats automated residual diagnostics as a first-class output and
introduces \emph{diagnostic-gated refitting}: if the Ljung-Box
white-noise test or ACF exceedance check fails for a BIC-selected
SARIMA model, the pipeline automatically increases the search order
(up to $p{=}q{=}5$, max 3 iterations) until diagnostics pass.
Evaluated on five real-world public series using a \emph{rolling-origin
protocol} (four expanding windows, 60--80\% training fraction) with
moving-block bootstrap confidence intervals and Harvey-corrected
Diebold-Mariano tests, FutureScope achieves an 85\% average
Ljung-Box pass rate across all 20 origin/series combinations; the
diagnostic-gated refitter is triggered on 15\% of origins.
Against four baselines—Prophet, ETS, Theta, and Seasonal Na\"{i}ve—
FutureScope wins on 3/5 series versus Prophet, \textbf{4/5 versus ETS},
and 3/5 versus Theta and Seasonal Na\"{i}ve, with two of the
Prophet comparisons reaching Bonferroni-adjusted significance
($\alpha{=}0.01$).  A fully reproducible benchmark
(\texttt{benchmark\_v2.py}, ${\approx}15$\,min on a laptop) and
Zenodo archive are provided.
}

\keywords{time series forecasting, residual diagnostics, SARIMA,
Ljung-Box test, diagnostic-gated refitting, rolling-origin evaluation,
moving-block bootstrap, Harvey-corrected DM test}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%============================================================

\subsection{The Diagnostic Gap in Forecasting Practice}

Modern time-series benchmarks—including the M4 Competition
\cite{makridakis2020m4} with 48{,}000 series and 61 methods—rank
methods exclusively by point-accuracy metrics (RMSE, SMAPE, MASE)
with no assessment of residual model assumptions.  This is
consequential: an ARIMA/SARIMA model that retains autocorrelated
residuals is formally \emph{mis-specified}.  Its confidence
intervals are invalid~\cite{box2015time}, and any downstream
decision relying on those intervals is statistically unsound.

\textbf{White-noise residuals} have zero mean, constant variance,
and no autocorrelation at non-zero lags.  SARIMA theory requires
white-noise residuals for Gauss-Markov optimality and for
$\chi^2$-based prediction intervals to be valid~\cite{box2015time}.

Model validation frameworks such as SR~11-7~\cite{sr117} recommend
residual checks as standard documentation.  FutureScope automates
these checks and, uniquely, \emph{acts on failure by refitting}.

\subsection{Related Work}

\textbf{Classical residual analysis.}
Box et al.~\cite{box2015time} and Hyndman and
Athanasopoulos~\cite{hyndman2018forecasting} recommend ACF inspection
and Ljung-Box testing as standard post-fit steps.  The \texttt{forecast}
R package~\cite{hyndman2018forecasting} exposes \texttt{checkresiduals()}
but does not trigger automatic refitting.

\textbf{Automated ARIMA.}
\texttt{pmdarima}~\cite{pmdarima} automates order selection but produces
no structured diagnostic output and performs no refitting based on
residual checks.  Auto-ETS in \texttt{statsmodels} similarly reports
no residual statistics.

\textbf{Neural forecasters.}
Transformer and LSTM-based methods~\cite{li2019enhancing} optimise
sequence losses and produce no residual diagnostic output by default.
Whether their residuals are white noise remains an open empirical
question outside the scope of this paper.

\textbf{FutureScope's contribution.}
To our knowledge, FutureScope is the first open-source Python pipeline
to (i)~treat residual diagnostics as a structured, required benchmark
output; (ii)~implement a \emph{diagnostic-gated refitting loop} that
automatically increases model complexity until residuals pass
white-noise checks; and (iii)~validate these claims with
rolling-origin evaluation and corrected statistical inference.

\subsection{Preprocessing Modes}

\textbf{Light mode} applies log-transform and first-order differencing.
\textbf{Full mode} additionally clips outliers (IQR rule), imputes
missing values, and applies min-max scaling.  Both modes are
evaluated in the preprocessing ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}).

\subsection{Automated Model Selection}

Seasonal period $s$ is set per dataset (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).
STL decomposition~\cite{cleveland1990stl} determines whether seasonal
SARIMA or non-seasonal ARIMA is fitted.
\texttt{pmdarima.auto\_arima}~\cite{pmdarima} uses a BIC stepwise
search with \texttt{max\_p}=\texttt{max\_q}=\texttt{max\_order}
(initially 2), \texttt{max\_d}=2.

\subsection{Diagnostic Validation}
\label{sec:diag}

Three statistics are computed on in-sample residuals
$\hat{\varepsilon}_t = y_t - \hat{y}_t$:

\subsubsection{Ljung-Box White-Noise Test}
\begin{equation}
  Q(m)=n(n{+}2)\sum_{k=1}^{m}\frac{\hat{\rho}_k^2}{n{-}k},
  \quad Q(m)\sim\chi^2(m)\text{ under }H_0.
  \label{eq:lb}
\end{equation}
We use $m{=}10$ lags; pass criterion $p{>}0.05$.
Implementation: \texttt{statsmodels.stats.diagnostic.acorr\_ljungbox}.

\subsubsection{ACF Exceedance}
The fraction of lags 1--20 whose sample ACF falls outside the
asymptotic 95\% pointwise bounds $\pm1.96/\sqrt{n}$.  Pass: $\leq 5\%$.

\subsubsection{Shapiro-Wilk Normality}
\texttt{scipy.stats.shapiro}; pass $p{>}0.05$; computed for $n\leq5{,}000$.

\subsection{Diagnostic-Gated Refitting}
\label{sec:refitting}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:refit} formalises the refitting procedure.
If either Ljung-Box or ACF exceedance fails, \texttt{max\_order}
is incremented by one and the model is refit, up to three iterations
or until \texttt{max\_order}~=~5.  The number of retries, whether
gating was triggered, and the final diagnostic statistics are all
recorded in the benchmark output.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
\caption{Diagnostic-Gated SARIMA Fitting}
\label{alg:refit}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require series $\mathbf{y}$, period $s$, \texttt{max\_order\_start}=2,
         \texttt{max\_order\_limit}=5, \texttt{max\_retries}=3
\State $q \leftarrow \texttt{max\_order\_start}$;
       \texttt{n\_retries} $\leftarrow 0$
\Repeat
  \State Fit SARIMA via BIC stepwise with $p,q \leq q$
  \State Compute $Q(10)$ (Ljung-Box) and ACF exceedance $f$
  \If{$p_{\rm LB} > 0.05$ \textbf{and} $f \leq 5\%$}
    \State \textbf{break} \Comment{diagnostics satisfied}
  \EndIf
  \State $q \leftarrow \min(q+1,\texttt{max\_order\_limit})$;
         \texttt{n\_retries}++
\Until{\texttt{n\_retries} $\geq$ \texttt{max\_retries}
       \textbf{or} $q \geq \texttt{max\_order\_limit}$}
\State \Return forecast, diagnostics, \texttt{n\_retries}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Rolling-Origin Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:rolling}

A single 80/20 split is insufficient to assess forecast robustness.
We use an expanding-window (rolling-origin) design with $K{=}4$
origins per series.  Training fractions are evenly spaced from
60\% to 80\% of $N$; the corresponding test sets cover 20\%--40\%.
All metrics are reported as \textbf{mean~$\pm$~std} across origins.

\subsection{Moving-Block Bootstrap for RMSE}
\label{sec:mbb}

The i.i.d.\ bootstrap ignores temporal dependence in forecast errors.
We replace it with a \textbf{moving-block bootstrap}~\cite{kunsch1989}:
errors $\{e_t\}$ are resampled in blocks of length
$b=\max(2,\lceil n^{1/3}\rceil)$ (data-driven rule-of-thumb~\cite{kunsch1989}),
drawn uniformly with replacement from all valid block starts.
1{,}000 resamples; 95\% CI from the 2.5th--97.5th percentiles.

\subsection{Harvey-Corrected Diebold-Mariano Test}
\label{sec:dm}

Let $d_t = e_{1,t}^2 - e_{2,t}^2$ be the squared-error loss
differential.  The test statistic uses a Newey-West HAC variance
estimator with Bartlett kernel and bandwidth
$b_w = \lfloor 4(n/100)^{2/9}\rfloor$:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\sigma}^2_{\rm HAC} =
  \hat{\gamma}_0 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{b_w}\!\left(1-\tfrac{k}{b_w{+}1}\right)\hat{\gamma}_k,
  \label{eq:hac}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\gamma}_k$ is the sample autocovariance at lag $k$.
The Harvey et al.~\cite{harvey1997} small-sample correction factor is
\begin{equation}
  c = \sqrt{\frac{n+1-2h+h(h-1)/n}{n}},\quad h=1.
  \label{eq:harvey}
\end{equation}
The corrected statistic $\widetilde{\rm DM} = c \cdot \bar{d} /
\sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2_{\rm HAC}/n}$ is referred to a $t_{n-1}$
distribution.  Multiple-testing correction: Bonferroni
$\alpha_{\rm adj}=0.01$ (five series per comparison set).

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol Summary}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Rolling origins}: $K{=}4$, training fraction
        $\in\{60\%,\,67\%,\,73\%,\,80\%\}$ of $N$.
  \item \textbf{Metric}: RMSE (primary), MAE (secondary).
        Reported as mean~$\pm$~std across origins.
  \item \textbf{Bootstrap CIs}: moving-block bootstrap,
        $b{=}\lceil n^{1/3}\rceil$, 1{,}000 resamples (Section~\ref{sec:mbb}).
  \item \textbf{Statistical test}: HAC+Harvey DM test
        (Section~\ref{sec:dm}); Bonferroni $\alpha_{\rm adj}{=}0.01$.
  \item \textbf{Refitting}: Algorithm~\ref{alg:refit};
        results recorded per origin.
  \item \textbf{Seed}: \texttt{numpy.random.seed(42)} throughout.
  \item \textbf{Hardware}: Windows~11 laptop, single CPU core,
        Python~3.11, \texttt{statsmodels}~0.14, \texttt{pmdarima}~2.x.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Effect of Diagnostic-Gated Refitting}
\label{sec:refit_effect}

For Bitcoin and Airline, where refitting was triggered, we analyse
the RMSE change between first-pass and final models.  The refitting
loop on Bitcoin (1.5 mean extra fits per triggered origin) did not
materially improve RMSE (mean change $<2\%$), confirming that the
diagnostic failure on Bitcoin reflects an inherent mis-specification
of linear SARIMA for cryptocurrency volatility rather than an
underfitting issue solvable by increasing order.  For Airline
(0.75 mean retries), the 25\% of origins that required refitting
passed diagnostics after one additional iteration with no RMSE
degradation, representing the ideal case: diagnostics improve
without accuracy loss.

%%============================================================
\section{Positioning and Scope of Contribution}
\label{sec:contribution}
%%============================================================

FutureScope's scientific contribution is threefold:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Diagnostic-gated refitting} (Algorithm~\ref{alg:refit}):
        the first open-source implementation of an automated loop that
        refits SARIMA models until residuals pass white-noise checks.
        On these five series, 15\% of rolling origins required
        refitting, demonstrating the mechanism is both necessary and
        functional.

  \item \textbf{Corrected statistical inference} for forecasting
        benchmarks: moving-block bootstrap (replacing i.i.d.)
        and HAC+Harvey DM tests (replacing uncorrected normal
        approximations) provide defensible inference under temporal
        dependence.

  \item \textbf{Reproducible diagnostic benchmarking}: structured
        output pairing residual statistics with accuracy metrics,
        enabling practitioners to evaluate \emph{both} model
        correctness and predictive accuracy in one run.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Regulatory alignment.}
SR~11-7~\cite{sr117} and similar model risk frameworks recommend
residual autocorrelation checks as standard model validation
documentation.  FutureScope's automated output reduces manual effort
for such documentation.  A full regulatory compliance assessment is
beyond this paper's scope.

\textbf{Scope limitation.}
All results are from five single hand-selected series.  The win rates
and diagnostic pass rates reported here should be treated as
exploratory case studies pending evaluation on larger corpora
(see Section~\ref{sec:future}).

%%============================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}
%%============================================================

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Five series}: exploratory case studies, not a
        statistically representative sample.
  \item \textbf{Linear SARIMA}: the diagnostic framework inherits
        the model family's limitations on high-volatility (Bitcoin)
        and multi-seasonal (M4 Hourly) series.
  \item \textbf{Refitting is not guaranteed}: Bitcoin's persistent
        diagnostic failure (50\% LB pass rate after refitting)
        shows that increasing SARIMA order cannot fix all
        mis-specifications.
  \item \textbf{Computational cost}: ${\approx}200\times$ slower than
        Prophet; unsuitable for real-time applications.
  \item \textbf{Univariate only}: no VAR/VARMA support.
\end{enumerate}

%%============================================================
\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
%%============================================================

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Large-scale evaluation}: apply the rolling-origin
        benchmark to the full M4 Hourly collection (414 series)
        to obtain statistically representative diagnostic pass rates
        and win rates.
  \item \textbf{Non-linear diagnostics}: ARCH/GARCH tests for
        conditional heteroskedasticity; cumulative sum (CUSUM) tests
        for structural breaks—relevant to Bitcoin and COVID-19 series.
  \item \textbf{Neural-forecaster diagnostics}: apply the diagnostic
        module to LSTM and Transformer residuals to empirically
        establish whether their residuals are white noise.
\end{enumerate}

%%============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%%============================================================

We presented FutureScope v2, an open-source SARIMA pipeline with
diagnostic-gated refitting, rolling-origin evaluation, moving-block
bootstrap confidence intervals, and Harvey-corrected DM tests.
On five real-world public series:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The \textbf{diagnostic-gated refitter is triggered on 15\%
        of rolling origins} across the five series, confirming that
        the mechanism addresses a real, non-trivial failure mode.
  \item After gating, the \textbf{average Ljung-Box pass rate
        is 85\%} and the ACF exceedance pass rate is \textbf{100\%}.
  \item FutureScope wins \textbf{3/5 versus Prophet},
        \textbf{4/5 versus ETS}, and 3/5 versus Theta and
        Seasonal Na\"{i}ve, with two Prophet comparisons
        reaching Bonferroni-adjusted significance.
  \item \textbf{Full preprocessing mode} materially reduces RMSE
        on the COVID-19 series ($-26.7\%$) by clipping epidemic
        spikes; Light and Full are otherwise equivalent.
\end{enumerate}

The central contribution is methodological: a reproducible,
open-source framework that pairs accuracy benchmarking with
principled residual validation and automatic remediation, providing
the statistical transparency that regulated forecasting applications
require and that current open-source tools do not offer.

\section{Statistical Implementation Details}
\label{app:stats}

\subsection{Moving-Block Bootstrap}
Block length $b=\max(2,\lceil n^{1/3}\rceil)$ (values: 4--6 across
series, reported in Table~\ref{tab:diag}).  At each of 1{,}000
resamples, $\lceil n/b\rceil$ blocks are drawn uniformly with
replacement from all valid start positions $\{0,\ldots,n{-}b\}$;
the concatenated sequence is truncated to length $n$ and RMSE is
computed.  Random state: \texttt{numpy.random.RandomState(42)}.

\subsection{HAC+Harvey DM Test}
Full specification: squared-error loss; Newey-West HAC bandwidth
$b_w{=}\lfloor4(n/100)^{2/9}\rfloor$ (values: 2--5 across series);
Bartlett kernel weights $w_k{=}1{-}k/(b_w{+}1)$; Harvey factor
as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:harvey} with $h{=}1$; reference distribution
$t_{n-1}$.  Bonferroni threshold $\alpha_{\rm adj}{=}0.01$
(five pairwise comparisons per baseline).

\subsection{Ljung-Box and ACF Diagnostics}
\texttt{acorr\_ljungbox(residuals, lags=[10])}: pass $p{>}0.05$.
\texttt{acf(residuals, nlags=20, alpha=0.05)}: exceedance = count of
lags 1--20 outside returned 95\% CI bounds, divided by 20; pass
$\leq5\%$.  Shapiro-Wilk: \texttt{scipy.stats.shapiro}; pass $p{>}0.05$;
only computed for $n\leq5{,}000$.
