\abstract{
Time series forecasting is fundamental to decision-making in finance,
epidemiology, and resource planning, yet most existing frameworks
evaluate models solely on point-accuracy metrics such as RMSE and MASE,
ignoring whether the fitted model is statistically well-specified.
Current tools like \texttt{pmdarima} and Prophet provide no structured
residual validation or automated corrective action upon diagnostic failure.
In this work, we propose \textbf{DGSAR}, a diagnostic-first
SARIMA forecasting pipeline that integrates residual validation directly
into the model selection loop.
Specifically, DGSAR implements a \emph{diagnostic-gated refitting}
mechanism: after each BIC-selected SARIMA fit, Ljung-Box and ACF
exceedance tests are evaluated, and the model is automatically refit
with increased order bounds until white-noise residual criteria are
satisfied.
We evaluate the proposed approach on five real-world public series
and 41 M4 Competition series using a rolling-origin protocol with
moving-block bootstrap confidence intervals and Harvey-corrected
Diebold-Mariano tests.
Experimental results show that DGSAR achieves an 85\% Ljung-Box
pass rate across all origin/series combinations, wins on 5/5 series
versus Prophet on MASE, and the gating mechanism improves diagnostic
quality without degrading point accuracy (<2\% RMSE change).
The proposed method provides a reproducible, open-source framework
that bridges the gap between predictive accuracy and statistical
model validity in time series forecasting. Recent surveys of modern
statistical and deep learning forecasters highlight precisely this
tension between accuracy and model adequacy~\cite{kim2025deeplearningsurvey}.
}
\keywords{time series forecasting, residual diagnostics, SARIMA,
Ljung-Box test, diagnostic-gated refitting, rolling-origin evaluation,
moving-block bootstrap, Harvey-corrected DM test}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%============================================================

Time series forecasting underpins critical decision-making across
domains including financial planning, epidemiological surveillance,
energy demand management, and supply chain
optimisation~\cite{hyndman2018forecasting, box2015time, kim2025deeplearningsurvey}.
Modern benchmarks such as the M4 Competition~\cite{makridakis2020m4},
which evaluated 61 methods on 100{,}000 series, and the subsequent
M5 retail forecasting competition~\cite{makridakis2022m5} have advanced the
field by establishing standardised accuracy comparisons.
Complementary repositories such as the Monash Time Series Forecasting
Archive and Repository~\cite{godahewa2021monash} provide curated multi-domain
datasets for broad benchmarking of both univariate and global models.

However, these benchmarks rank methods exclusively by point-accuracy
metrics (RMSE, SMAPE, MASE) with \emph{no assessment of residual
model assumptions}. This is consequential: an ARIMA/SARIMA model
whose residuals exhibit autocorrelation is formally
\emph{mis-specified}---its confidence intervals are
invalid~\cite{box2015time}, and downstream decisions relying on
uncertainty quantification become statistically unsound.
Standard order selection procedures based on information criteria
such as AIC and BIC~\cite{akaike1974aic, schwarz1978bic} are powerful,
but they target in-sample fit rather than explicit residual
whiteness.
Existing automated tools such as \texttt{pmdarima}~\cite{pmdarima}
and the \texttt{forecast} package's \texttt{auto.arima}~\cite{hyndman2008forecast}
optimise model selection but produce no structured residual validation
and take no corrective action upon diagnostic failure.
Recent work on forecast evaluation further emphasises that robust
rolling-origin design and blocked cross-validation are necessary
to avoid misleading conclusions about model performance~\cite{tashman2000oos, bergmeir2012cv, hewamalage2023pitfalls}.

The rise of deep learning forecasters---from recurrent architectures
such as DeepAR~\cite{salinas2020deepar} and N-BEATS~\cite{oreshkin2019nbeats}
to Transformer-based methods including Informer~\cite{zhou2021informer},
Autoformer~\cite{wu2021autoformer}, and PatchTST~\cite{nie2022patchtst},
and more recently foundation models such as Chronos~\cite{ansari2024chronos}
and TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm}---has further widened the diagnostic
gap.  These models are trained end-to-end on sequence losses and
produce no structured residual output, making it impossible to
assess whether their predictions rest on statistically sound model
assumptions~\cite{kim2025deeplearningsurvey, kottapalli2025survey}.

In this work, we propose \textbf{DGSAR} (Diagnostic-Gated SARIMA), a diagnostic-first
forecasting framework that closes this gap by integrating residual
validation directly into the SARIMA model selection loop.
The key contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A \textbf{diagnostic-gated refitting algorithm} that
automatically increases SARIMA order bounds when Ljung-Box
or ACF exceedance tests fail, ensuring residual white-noise
compliance without manual intervention. Our implementation
follows best practices on lag choice and test power trade-offs
for Ljung--Box statistics~\cite{ljungbox1978, hassani2020lb}.
\item An \textbf{extended diagnostic suite} incorporating ARCH-LM
testing to distinguish mean mis-specification from volatility
clustering, providing prescriptive guidance on model class
selection, in line with GARCH theory~\cite{engle1982, bollerslev1986garch}.
\item A \textbf{rigorous evaluation protocol} using rolling-origin
design, moving-block bootstrap confidence intervals, and
Harvey-corrected Diebold-Mariano tests, validated on five
real-world series and 41 M4 Competition series, consistent
with modern guidance on forecast evaluation and diagnostics~\cite{tashman2000oos, hewamalage2023pitfalls}.
\item A \textbf{fully reproducible, open-source implementation}
with structured diagnostic output suitable for regulated
forecasting environments (e.g., SR~11-7~\cite{sr117} and recent
ECB guidance on internal models and model risk~\cite{ecb2024guide}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%%============================================================

\textit{Classical residual analysis.}
Box et al.~\cite{box2015time} and Hyndman and
Athanasopoulos~\cite{hyndman2018forecasting} recommend ACF inspection
and Ljung-Box testing as standard post-fit steps. The \texttt{forecast}
R package~\cite{hyndman2018forecasting} exposes \texttt{checkresiduals()}
but does not trigger automatic refitting, and more recent work has
examined how the choice of Ljung--Box lag order affects size and power~\cite{hassani2020lb}.
Guidance from the forecast evaluation literature~\cite{tashman2000oos, bergmeir2012cv, hewamalage2023pitfalls}
also stresses that diagnostics should be embedded in robust evaluation
setups such as rolling-origin experiments and blocked cross-validation.

\textit{Automated ARIMA.}
\texttt{pmdarima}~\cite{pmdarima} automates order selection but produces
no structured diagnostic output and performs no refitting based on
residual checks. Closely related work in the R ecosystem,
\texttt{auto.arima} in the \texttt{forecast} package~\cite{hyndman2008forecast},
uses stepwise AIC/BIC optimisation~\cite{akaike1974aic, schwarz1978bic}
over ARIMA families, again without gating on residual whiteness.
Auto-ETS in \texttt{statsmodels} similarly reports
no residual statistics, despite the extensive modelling theory
for ETS~\cite{gardner2006exp} and its strong performance in competition
settings~\cite{makridakis2020m4, assimakopoulos2000theta}.

\textit{Neural forecasters.}
Deep learning methods such as N-BEATS and N-HiTS~\cite{oreshkin2019nbeats, challu2022nhits},
DeepAR~\cite{salinas2020deepar}, and Temporal Fusion Transformers~\cite{lim2021tft}
have shown strong performance on competition datasets including M3, M4 and TOURISM.
Transformer-based architectures tailored for long-horizon forecasting,
including Informer~\cite{zhou2021informer}, Autoformer~\cite{wu2021autoformer},
PatchTST~\cite{nie2022patchtst}, and iTransformer~\cite{zhou2023itransformer},
further refine attention mechanisms and patching strategies for multivariate
series. More recent work reprograms or adapts large language models
to time series forecasting via Time-LLM~\cite{jin2023timellm} and
related LLM-based forecasters. These models optimise sequence losses
and produce no residual diagnostic output by default; whether their
residuals are white noise remains an open empirical question outside
the scope of this paper~\cite{li2019enhancing, hewamalage2023pitfalls}.

\textit{Time-series foundation models.}
Chronos and related families pretrain transformer architectures on
tokenised time series to enable zero-shot and few-shot probabilistic
forecasting~\cite{ansari2024chronos}.
Decoder-only foundation models such as TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm}
and subsequent surveys and empirical studies~\cite{kottapalli2025survey, achour2025conformal}
examine their behaviour across benchmarks including the Monash archive~\cite{godahewa2021monash}
and M-series competitions~\cite{makridakis2020m4, makridakis2022m5}.
Recent work on efficient and domain-specific TSFMs~\cite{fu2026reverso, karaouli2025foundational, rahimikia2025finance, yu2025pmf}
highlights both the promise and limitations of foundation models relative
to specialised forecasters; however, none of these systems expose
structured residual diagnostics or turn diagnostics into a gating
criterion as DGSAR does.

\textit{AutoML for time series.}
AutoML frameworks such as AutoGluon--TimeSeries~\cite{shchur2023autogluon}
and broader surveys of ML and AutoML solutions for time-series
forecasting~\cite{alsharef2022automlreview} automate model selection and
hyperparameter tuning across large model zoos. Azure ML and other cloud
platforms also offer AutoML forecasting pipelines with advanced search
spaces and engineering heuristics. These systems prioritise accuracy
and robustness but typically treat residual diagnostics as optional,
manual post-processing rather than a first-class gating mechanism.

\textit{Hybrid and domain-specific approaches.}
Recent hybrid methods combine statistical and machine learning
components for improved forecasting of seasonal and chaotic
series~\cite{abbasimehr2024hybrid}, while domain-specific studies have
examined foundation model performance in finance~\cite{rahimikia2025finance}
and process mining~\cite{yu2025pmf}.  Conformal prediction
techniques have also been applied to foundation model
outputs~\cite{achour2025conformal}.  However, none of these approaches
incorporate structured residual diagnostics as a feedback mechanism
within the model fitting loop.

\textit{Research gap.}
Existing tools automate model \emph{selection} but not model
\emph{validation}.  No open-source Python pipeline treats residual
diagnostics as a gating criterion that triggers automated corrective
refitting.  DGSAR addresses this gap directly by embedding
diagnostic checks and refitting into the SARIMA order search,
providing both diagnostic transparency and prescriptive guidance
on model class adequacy.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}
%%============================================================

Given a univariate time series $\{y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_N\}$ with
potential trend, seasonality of period $s$, and stochastic components,
the objective is to produce $h$-step-ahead forecasts
$\{\hat{y}_{N+1}, \ldots, \hat{y}_{N+h}\}$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Point accuracy}: the forecast error
$e_t = y_t - \hat{y}_t$ is minimised under standard metrics
(RMSE, MAE, MASE);
\item \textbf{Statistical validity}: the in-sample residuals
$\hat{\varepsilon}_t = y_t - \hat{y}_t$ satisfy the white-noise
assumption, i.e., $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\varepsilon}_t] = 0$,
$\text{Var}(\hat{\varepsilon}_t) = \sigma^2$ (constant), and
$\text{Cov}(\hat{\varepsilon}_t, \hat{\varepsilon}_{t-k}) = 0$
for all $k \neq 0$.
\end{enumerate}

The SARIMA$(p,d,q)\times(P,D,Q)_s$ model represents $y_t$ as:
\begin{equation}
\Phi_P(B^s)\,\phi_p(B)\,(1{-}B)^d(1{-}B^s)^D\,y_t
= \Theta_Q(B^s)\,\theta_q(B)\,\varepsilon_t,
\quad \varepsilon_t \sim \text{WN}(0,\sigma^2),
\label{eq:sarima}
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the backshift operator, $\phi_p(B)$ and $\Phi_P(B^s)$ are
non-seasonal and seasonal AR polynomials, $\theta_q(B)$ and
$\Theta_Q(B^s)$ are MA polynomials, and $d$, $D$ are differencing orders.

\textit{The diagnostic gap.}
Standard automated pipelines (e.g., \texttt{pmdarima}) select orders
$(p,d,q,P,D,Q)$ via information criteria (BIC)~\cite{akaike1974aic, schwarz1978bic, hyndman2008forecast}
but do \emph{not} verify whether the fitted residuals $\hat{\varepsilon}_t$ satisfy the
white-noise assumption in Eq.~\eqref{eq:sarima}. When this assumption
is violated, confidence intervals become invalid~\cite{box2015time}
and downstream decisions based on uncertainty quantification are
statistically unsound. DGSAR formalises this validation step
as a \emph{diagnostic gate} within the fitting loop
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:refit}), leveraging insights from the residual
diagnostics literature on Ljung--Box lag choice and power~\cite{ljungbox1978, hassani2020lb}
and modern guidance on forecast evaluation design~\cite{tashman2000oos, hewamalage2023pitfalls}.

The six stages are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Data Ingestion \& Validation}: loading, type checking,
missing-value detection, and frequency inference.
\item \textbf{Adaptive Preprocessing}: Light mode (log-transform,
differencing) or Full mode (additionally: IQR outlier clipping,
imputation, min-max scaling).
\item \textbf{STL Decomposition}: seasonal-trend decomposition
using LOESS~\cite{cleveland1990stl} to determine whether
seasonal SARIMA or non-seasonal ARIMA is appropriate.
\item \textbf{Automated Model Selection}: BIC-stepwise search via
\texttt{pmdarima}~\cite{pmdarima} with configurable order bounds,
consistent with classical information-criteria-based order selection~\cite{akaike1974aic, schwarz1978bic, hyndman2008forecast}.
\item \textbf{MLE Forecasting}: maximum likelihood estimation of
selected SARIMA parameters and $h$-step-ahead prediction.
\item \textbf{Diagnostic Suite \& Gated Refitting}: Ljung-Box,
ACF exceedance, Shapiro-Wilk, and ARCH-LM tests on residuals;
automatic refitting if white-noise criteria fail
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:refit}), following recommendations on lag
selection and power of portmanteau tests~\cite{ljungbox1978, hassani2020lb}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Preprocessing Modes}

\textbf{Light mode} applies log-transform and first-order differencing.
\textbf{Full mode} additionally clips outliers (IQR rule), imputes
missing values, and applies min-max scaling. Both modes are
evaluated in the preprocessing ablation (Section~\ref{sec:preproc_ablation}).

\subsection{Automated Model Selection}

Seasonal period $s$ is set per dataset (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).
STL decomposition~\cite{cleveland1990stl} determines whether seasonal
SARIMA or non-seasonal ARIMA is fitted.
\texttt{pmdarima.auto\_arima}~\cite{pmdarima} uses a BIC stepwise
search with \texttt{max\_p}=\texttt{max\_q}=\texttt{max\_order}
(initially 2), \texttt{max\_d}=2, analogous to the
\texttt{auto.arima} procedure in the \texttt{forecast} package~\cite{hyndman2008forecast}.

\subsection{Diagnostic Validation}
\label{sec:diag}

Three statistics are computed on in-sample residuals
$\hat{\varepsilon}_t = y_t - \hat{y}_t$:

\subsubsection{Ljung-Box White-Noise Test}
\begin{equation}
Q(m)=n(n{+}2)\sum_{k=1}^{m}\frac{\hat{\rho}_k^2}{n{-}k},
\quad Q(m)\sim\chi^2(m)\text{ under }H_0.
\label{eq:lb}
\end{equation}
We use $m{=}10$ lags; pass criterion $p{>}0.05$.
Implementation: \texttt{statsmodels.stats.diagnostic.acorr\_ljungbox}.
The impact of the lag order $m$ on test size and power has been
studied in detail~\cite{hassani2020lb}, and our choice reflects
a balance between power and robustness for the series lengths considered.

\subsubsection{ACF Exceedance}
The fraction of lags 1--20 whose sample ACF falls outside the
asymptotic 95\% pointwise bounds $\pm1.96/\sqrt{n}$. Pass: $\leq 5\%$.

\subsubsection{Shapiro-Wilk Normality}
\texttt{scipy.stats.shapiro}; pass $p{>}0.05$; computed for $n\leq5{,}000$.

\subsection{Diagnostic-Gated Refitting}
\label{sec:refitting}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:refit} formalises the refitting procedure.
If either Ljung-Box or ACF exceedance fails, \texttt{max\_order}
is incremented by one and the model is refit, up to three iterations
or until \texttt{max\_order}~=~5. The number of retries, whether
gating was triggered, and the final diagnostic statistics are all
recorded in the benchmark output.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
\caption{Diagnostic-Gated SARIMA Fitting}
\label{alg:refit}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require series $\mathbf{y}$, period $s$, \texttt{max\_order\_start}=2,
\texttt{max\_order\_limit}=5, \texttt{max\_retries}=3
\State $q \leftarrow \texttt{max\_order\_start}$;
\texttt{n\_retries} $\leftarrow 0$
\Repeat
\State Fit SARIMA via BIC stepwise with $p,q \leq q$
\State Compute $Q(10)$ (Ljung-Box) and ACF exceedance $f$
\If{$p_{\rm LB} > 0.05$ \textbf{and} $f \leq 5\%$}
\State \textbf{break} \Comment{diagnostics satisfied}
\EndIf
\State $q \leftarrow \min(q+1,\texttt{max\_order\_limit})$;
\texttt{n\_retries}++
\Until{\texttt{n\_retries} $\geq$ \texttt{max\_retries}
\textbf{or} $q \geq \texttt{max\_order\_limit}$}
\State \Return forecast, diagnostics, \texttt{n\_retries}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Rolling-Origin Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:rolling}

A single 80/20 split is insufficient to assess forecast robustness.
We use an expanding-window (rolling-origin) design with $K{=}4$
origins per series. Training fractions are evenly spaced from
60\% to 80\% of $N$; the corresponding test sets cover 20\%--40\%.
All metrics are reported as \textbf{mean~$\pm$~std} across origins,
in line with guidance on out-of-sample tests, diversity and
adequacy in forecast evaluation~\cite{tashman2000oos, bergmeir2012cv, hewamalage2023pitfalls}.

\subsection{Moving-Block Bootstrap for RMSE}
\label{sec:mbb}

The i.i.d.\ bootstrap ignores temporal dependence in forecast errors.
We replace it with a \textbf{moving-block bootstrap}~\cite{kunsch1989},
and discuss it relative to alternative block-based approaches such as
the stationary bootstrap~\cite{politis1994stationary}.
Errors $\{e_t\}$ are resampled in blocks of length
$b=\max(2,\lceil n^{1/3}\rceil)$ (data-driven rule-of-thumb~\cite{kunsch1989}),
drawn uniformly with replacement from all valid block starts.
1{,}000 resamples; 95\% CI from the 2.5th--97.5th percentiles.

\subsection{Harvey-Corrected Diebold-Mariano Test}
\label{sec:dm}

Let $d_t = e_{1,t}^2 - e_{2,t}^2$ be the squared-error loss
differential. The test statistic uses a Newey-West HAC variance
estimator with Bartlett kernel and bandwidth
$b_w = \lfloor 4(n/100)^{2/9}\rfloor$:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\sigma}^2_{\rm HAC} =
\hat{\gamma}_0 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{b_w}\!\left(1-\tfrac{k}{b_w{+}1}\right)\hat{\gamma}_k,
\label{eq:hac}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\gamma}_k$ is the sample autocovariance at lag $k$.
The Harvey et al.~\cite{harvey1997} small-sample correction factor is
\begin{equation}
c = \sqrt{\frac{n+1-2h+h(h-1)/n}{n}},\quad h=1.
\label{eq:harvey}
\end{equation}
The corrected statistic $\widetilde{\rm DM} = c \cdot \bar{d} /
\sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2_{\rm HAC}/n}$ is referred to a $t_{n-1}$
distribution. Multiple-testing correction: Bonferroni
$\alpha_{\rm adj}=0.01$ (five series per comparison set).

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol Summary}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Rolling origins}: $K{=}4$, training fraction
$\in\{60\%,\,67\%,\,73\%,\,80\%\}$ of $N$, following
rolling-origin and sliding-window recommendations~\cite{tashman2000oos, bergmeir2012cv, hewamalage2023pitfalls}.
\item \textbf{Metric}: RMSE (primary), MAE (secondary).
Reported as mean~$\pm$~std across origins.
\item \textbf{Bootstrap CIs}: moving-block bootstrap,
$b{=}\lceil n^{1/3}\rceil$, 1{,}000 resamples (Section~\ref{sec:mbb}),
inspired by block and stationary bootstrap methods~\cite{kunsch1989, politis1994stationary}.
\item \textbf{Statistical test}: HAC+Harvey DM test
(Section~\ref{sec:dm}); Bonferroni $\alpha_{\rm adj}{=}0.01$.
\item \textbf{Refitting}: Algorithm~\ref{alg:refit};
results recorded per origin.
\item \textbf{Seed}: \texttt{numpy.random.seed(42)} throughout.
\item \textbf{Hardware}: Windows~11 laptop, single CPU core,
Python~3.11, \texttt{statsmodels}~0.14, \texttt{pmdarima}~2.x.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

The following metrics are used to assess forecast quality:

\textit{Root Mean Squared Error} (RMSE):
\begin{equation}
  \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{h}\sum_{t=1}^{h}(y_t - \hat{y}_t)^2},
  \label{eq:rmse}
\end{equation}
where $h$ is the forecast horizon, $y_t$ is the observed value, and $\hat{y}_t$ is the forecast.

\textit{Mean Absolute Error} (MAE):
\begin{equation}
  \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{h}\sum_{t=1}^{h}|y_t - \hat{y}_t|.
  \label{eq:mae}
\end{equation}

\textit{Mean Absolute Scaled Error} (MASE), a scale-free metric enabling
cross-series comparison:
\begin{equation}
  \text{MASE} = \frac{\text{MAE}}{\frac{1}{N-s}\sum_{t=s+1}^{N}|y_t - y_{t-s}|},
  \label{eq:mase}
\end{equation}
where the denominator is the in-sample MAE of the seasonal na\"{i}ve
baseline with period $s$.  MASE $< 1$ indicates the model outperforms
the seasonal na\"{i}ve benchmark.

\subsection{Statistical Validation}
\label{sec:statval}

The following statistical procedures underpin the evaluation protocol:

\textit{Moving-Block Bootstrap.}
Block length $b=\max(2,\lceil n^{1/3}\rceil)$ (values: 4--6 across
series).  At each of 1{,}000 resamples, $\lceil n/b\rceil$ blocks
are drawn uniformly with replacement from all valid start positions
$\{0,\ldots,n{-}b\}$; the concatenated sequence is truncated to
length $n$ and RMSE is computed.
Random state: \texttt{numpy.random.RandomState(42)}.
Alternative choices such as the stationary
bootstrap~\cite{politis1994stationary} could also be integrated.

\textit{HAC+Harvey DM Test.}
Full specification: squared-error loss; Newey-West HAC bandwidth
$b_w{=}\lfloor4(n/100)^{2/9}\rfloor$ (values: 2--5 across series);
Bartlett kernel weights $w_k{=}1{-}k/(b_w{+}1)$; Harvey factor
as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:harvey} with $h{=}1$; reference distribution
$t_{n-1}$.  Bonferroni threshold $\alpha_{\rm adj}{=}0.01$
(five pairwise comparisons per baseline).

\textit{Ljung-Box and ACF Diagnostics.}
\texttt{acorr\_ljungbox(residuals, lags=[10])}: pass $p{>}0.05$.
\texttt{acf(residuals, nlags=20, alpha=0.05)}: exceedance = count of
lags 1--20 outside returned 95\% CI bounds, divided by 20; pass
$\leq5\%$.  Shapiro-Wilk: \texttt{scipy.stats.shapiro}; pass $p{>}0.05$;
only computed for $n\leq5{,}000$.

\textit{Why ACF and Ljung-Box can disagree.}
Although both tests probe residual autocorrelation, they have
different sensitivity profiles.
The ACF exceedance check tests each of lags 1--20
\emph{individually} against the asymptotic 95\% pointwise band
$\pm 1.96/\sqrt{n}$; it fails only when $\geq 6$ of those 20 lags
individually breach the band.
The Ljung-Box $Q(10)$ statistic aggregates squared autocorrelations
over 10 lags into a single $\chi^2$ test: even if no single lag
exceeds the pointwise band by much, the \emph{joint} contribution
of several mildly elevated lags can push $Q(10)$ past its critical
value~\cite{ljungbox1978, hassani2020lb}.
On Daily (D1), COVID-19, and Airline, this is the observed pattern:
individual ACF values pass at 100\% while Ljung-Box rejects in 25\%
of origins, indicating diffuse, low-amplitude autocorrelation spread
across multiple lags. The gating mechanism triggers refitting to
address this; residual autocorrelation is reduced but not fully
eliminated at the chosen order bounds.

\textit{ARCH-LM test for volatility clustering.}
To characterise \emph{why} SARIMA fails on certain series,
Table~\ref{tab:diag} includes an ARCH-LM test~\cite{engle1982}
on the fitted residuals (\texttt{statsmodels.stats.diagnostic.het\_arch},
$L{=}5$ lags). A failing ARCH test ($p{\leq}0.05$)
indicates that residual variance is autocorrelated, i.e., the series
exhibits volatility clustering that SARIMA---a model of the conditional
mean only---cannot remove. This is qualitatively distinct from
Ljung-Box failure, which indicates autocorrelation in the mean.
When ARCH passes but Ljung-Box fails, practitioners should increase
SARIMA order (the gating loop helps); when ARCH itself fails,
a GARCH-ARIMA model is the appropriate remedy~\cite{bollerslev1986garch}.
Results confirm the hypothesis: Bitcoin (ARCH 100\%) and COVID-19
(ARCH 100\%) fail the ARCH test, revealing volatility clustering that
SARIMA cannot address regardless of order. Airline exhibits moderate
volatility clustering (ARCH 75\%). M4~Hourly and Daily (D1) pass
ARCH (0\%), confirming their residual structure is mean-type only
and addressable by the gating mechanism.

\textit{Refitting in practice.}
The gating mechanism is triggered on 30\% of origins overall.
Daily (D1) requires refitting most (75\% of its origins), followed
by COVID-19 (50\%) and Airline (25\%).
The refitting loop improves but does not always eliminate residual
autocorrelation: Daily (D1) and COVID-19 remain at 75\% LB pass rate
after gating, indicating persistent autocorrelation at the selected
order bounds ($p_{\max}{=}5$).
Bitcoin, by contrast, passes LB at 100\% without any refitting---its
SARIMA mean structure is well-specified---but fails ARCH at 100\%,
confirming that cryptocurrency volatility is heteroskedastic in origin
and lies outside SARIMA's remediation scope, motivating future
extensions that integrate GARCH components~\cite{bollerslev1986garch}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%%============================================================

\subsection{Interpretation of Results}

The experimental results demonstrate that DGSAR's
diagnostic-gated refitting mechanism addresses a real, non-trivial
failure mode: 30\% of rolling origins across the five case-study
series triggered the refitting loop. Crucially, the ablation
against ungated SARIMA (DGSAR\,NG in Table~\ref{tab:mase}) confirms
that gating improves diagnostic validity \emph{without degrading
point accuracy} ($<2\%$ RMSE change across all series). This
establishes that the contribution is \textbf{diagnostic certification},
not predictive gain, complementing rather than competing with recent
advances in neural and transformer-based forecasting~\cite{oreshkin2019nbeats, challu2022nhits, salinas2020deepar, lim2021tft, zhou2021informer, wu2021autoformer, nie2022patchtst, zhou2023itransformer, ansari2024chronos, das2024timesfm, jin2023timellm, kim2025deeplearningsurvey}.

On point accuracy, DGSAR wins 3/5 series versus Prophet and
4/5 versus ETS on RMSE; on MASE (scale-free), the win rate rises
to 5/5 versus Prophet. These accuracy gains are a by-product of
fitting a well-specified SARIMA model, not the primary objective.
On series where SARIMA is inherently inadequate (M4~Hourly:
multi-seasonal structure; Bitcoin: heteroskedastic volatility), no
diagnostic-driven accuracy improvement is expected or claimed.

\subsection{Diagnostic Prescriptiveness}

The ARCH-LM test (Table~\ref{tab:diag}) provides a prescriptive
diagnostic layer that goes beyond pass/fail reporting. By
distinguishing mean mis-specification (Ljung-Box failure, addressable
by increasing SARIMA order) from variance mis-specification (ARCH
failure, requiring GARCH), DGSAR guides practitioners toward
the correct model class~\cite{engle1982, bollerslev1986garch}.
Bitcoin and COVID-19 both exhibit 100\%
ARCH failure, correctly classifying them as requiring
GARCH-ARIMA---a qualitatively different remedy from order escalation.

\subsection{Computational Considerations}

DGSAR is approximately $200\times$ slower than Prophet in total
pipeline time. However, the runtime is dominated by ARIMA order
search, not diagnostics: the diagnostic suite (LB, ACF, ARCH,
Shapiro-Wilk) adds $<1\%$ of total per-origin time. The computational
overhead is the cost of SARIMA model fitting, not the diagnostic
framework itself, and is relevant only for latency-sensitive
applications. Similar accuracy-versus-cost trade-offs have been
documented for deep learning forecasters and time-series foundation
models~\cite{oreshkin2019nbeats, challu2022nhits, zhou2021informer, wu2021autoformer, ansari2024chronos, das2024timesfm, fu2026reverso, karaouli2025foundational, rahimikia2025finance, yu2025pmf, kottapalli2025survey}.

\subsection{Regulatory and Practical Implications}

Model risk management frameworks such as SR~11-7~\cite{sr117}
recommend residual autocorrelation checks as standard model validation
documentation, and recent ECB guidance on internal models explicitly
calls for enterprise-wide model risk management, including non-regulatory
models~\cite{ecb2024guide}. DGSAR's automated, structured diagnostic output
reduces the manual effort required for such documentation, making it
particularly suitable for regulated forecasting environments in
finance, insurance, and healthcare.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Limited evaluation scope}: the five case-study series
and 41 M4 series are exploratory; results should be validated
on larger, more diverse corpora such as those surveyed in recent
deep learning and foundation-model reviews~\cite{kim2025deeplearningsurvey, kottapalli2025survey}.
\item \textbf{Linear SARIMA assumption}: the diagnostic framework
inherits the model family's limitations on high-volatility
and multi-seasonal series.
\item \textbf{Refitting is not guaranteed to succeed}: Bitcoin's
persistent diagnostic failure shows that increasing SARIMA
order cannot address all forms of mis-specification.
\item \textbf{Computational cost}: ${\approx}200\times$ slower than
Prophet; unsuitable for real-time or large-scale applications.
\item \textbf{Univariate only}: no support for multivariate
(VAR/VARMA) modelling, in contrast to some modern
transformer-based and foundation-model forecasters~\cite{lim2021tft, zhou2021informer, wu2021autoformer, nie2022patchtst, zhou2023itransformer, ansari2024chronos, das2024timesfm, fu2026reverso}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%%============================================================

We presented DGSAR, an open-source diagnostic-first SARIMA
forecasting pipeline that integrates residual validation into the
model selection loop. The key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The \textbf{diagnostic-gated refitting} mechanism is triggered
on 30\% of rolling origins, confirming it addresses a real,
non-trivial failure mode. After gating, the average Ljung-Box
pass rate is \textbf{85\%} (Wilson 95\%~CI: [63\%, 95\%]) and
the ACF exceedance pass rate is \textbf{100\%}.
\item ARCH-LM testing reveals that Bitcoin and COVID-19 exhibit
\textbf{100\% volatility clustering}, correctly classifying
them as requiring GARCH rather than higher-order SARIMA---a
prescriptive diagnostic output absent from existing tools~\cite{engle1982, bollerslev1986garch}.
\item On MASE (scale-free), DGSAR wins \textbf{5/5 versus
Prophet}, 3/5 versus ETS, and 3/5 versus Theta, with the
no-gating ablation achieving identical MASE, confirming that
diagnostic gating adds no accuracy cost.
\item On the expanded M4 subset (41 series), LB pass rates reach
\textbf{100\% for Yearly} and 75--90\% for Monthly/Quarterly,
with overall MASE win rates of 75.6\% versus Prophet.
\end{enumerate}

The central contribution is methodological: a reproducible framework
that pairs accuracy benchmarking with principled residual validation
and automatic remediation, providing the statistical transparency
that regulated forecasting applications require. It is complementary
to, rather than a replacement for, ongoing advances in neural and
foundation-model forecasting~\cite{oreshkin2019nbeats, challu2022nhits, salinas2020deepar, lim2021tft, zhou2021informer, wu2021autoformer, nie2022patchtst, zhou2023itransformer, ansari2024chronos, das2024timesfm, jin2023timellm, kottapalli2025survey, achour2025conformal, fu2026reverso, karaouli2025foundational, rahimikia2025finance, kim2025deeplearningsurvey}.

\subsection{Future Directions}
Several extensions are planned to address the current limitations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{GARCH-ARIMA integration}: when ARCH-LM fails,
automatically switch to a GARCH(1,1)-ARIMA model instead of
escalating SARIMA order, extending the diagnostic-gated
framework to handle variance mis-specification~\cite{engle1982, bollerslev1986garch}.
\item \textbf{Structural break detection}: incorporating CUSUM
tests for regime changes in series such as COVID-19 and
Bitcoin, where non-stationarity cannot be addressed by order
escalation alone.
\item \textbf{Neural forecaster diagnostics}: applying the LB +
ARCH diagnostic pipeline to LSTM and Transformer residuals
to assess whether deep learning and foundation-model
forecasters~\cite{oreshkin2019nbeats, challu2022nhits, salinas2020deepar, lim2021tft, zhou2021informer, wu2021autoformer, nie2022patchtst, zhou2023itransformer, ansari2024chronos, das2024timesfm, jin2023timellm, fu2026reverso, kottapalli2025survey, achour2025conformal, karaouli2025foundational, rahimikia2025finance, kim2025deeplearningsurvey}
produce white-noise residuals.
\item \textbf{Large-scale evaluation}: scaling the benchmark to
the full M4 and M5 corpora and Monash Archive with all baseline
comparisons to establish population-level diagnostic and accuracy statistics~\cite{makridakis2020m4, makridakis2022m5, godahewa2021monash}.
\end{enumerate}
